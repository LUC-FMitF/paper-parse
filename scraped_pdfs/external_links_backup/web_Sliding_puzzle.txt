<p>A <b>sliding puzzle</b>, <b>sliding block puzzle</b>, or <b>sliding tile puzzle</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Combination_puzzle" title="Combination puzzle">combination puzzle</a> that challenges a player to slide (frequently flat) pieces along certain routes (usually on a board) to establish a certain end-configuration.  The pieces to be moved may consist of simple shapes, or they may be imprinted with colours, patterns, sections of a larger picture (like a jigsaw puzzle), numbers, or letters.
</p><p>Sliding puzzles are essentially two-dimensional in nature, even if the sliding is facilitated by mechanically interlinked pieces (like partially encaged marbles) or three-dimensional tokens. In manufactured wood and plastic products, the linking and encaging is often achieved in combination, through <a href="/wiki/Mortise_and_tenon" title="Mortise and tenon">mortise-and-tenon</a> key channels along the edges of the pieces. In at least one vintage case of the popular <a href="/wiki/Klotski#Huarong_Dao" title="Klotski">Chinese cognate game Huarong Road</a>, a wire screen prevents lifting of the pieces, which remain loose. As the illustration shows, some sliding puzzles are <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_puzzles" class="mw-redirect" title="Mechanical puzzles">mechanical puzzles</a>. However, the mechanical fixtures are usually not essential to these puzzles; the parts could as well be tokens on a flat board that are moved according to certain rules.
</p><p>Unlike <a href="/wiki/Tour_puzzle" title="Tour puzzle">tour puzzles</a>, a sliding block puzzle prohibits lifting any pieces off the board. This property separates sliding puzzles from <a href="/wiki/Rearrangement_puzzle" class="mw-redirect" title="Rearrangement puzzle">rearrangement puzzles</a>. Hence, finding moves and the paths opened up by each move within the two-dimensional confines of the board are important parts of solving sliding block puzzles.
</p><p>The oldest type of sliding puzzle is the <a href="/wiki/Fifteen_puzzle" class="mw-redirect" title="Fifteen puzzle">fifteen puzzle</a>, invented by Noyes Chapman in 1880; <a href="/wiki/Sam_Loyd" title="Sam Loyd">Sam Loyd</a> is often wrongly credited with making sliding puzzles popular based on his false claim that he invented the fifteen puzzle.  Chapman's invention initiated a puzzle craze in the early 1880s.
From the 1950s through the 1980s sliding puzzles employing letters to form words were very popular.  These sorts of puzzles have several possible solutions, as may be seen from examples such as <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ro-Let&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Ro-Let (page does not exist)">Ro-Let</a> (a letter-based fifteen puzzle), <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scribe-o&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Scribe-o (page does not exist)">Scribe-o</a> (4x8), and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lingo_(puzzle)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lingo (puzzle) (page does not exist)">Lingo</a>.<sup id="cite&#95;ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span class="cite-bracket">&#91;</span>1<span class="cite-bracket">&#93;</span></a></sup>
</p><p>The fifteen puzzle has been computerized (as <a href="/wiki/Puzzle_video_game" title="Puzzle video game">puzzle video games</a>) and examples are available to play for free online from many Web pages. It is a descendant of the <a href="/wiki/Jigsaw_puzzle" title="Jigsaw puzzle">jigsaw puzzle</a> in that its point is to form a picture on-screen. The last square of the puzzle is then displayed automatically once the other pieces have been lined up.
</p>
